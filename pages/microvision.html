Microvision

Description

The Microvision was the first handheld video game system that used interchangeable cartridges. The Microvision was designed by Jay Smith and his company Smith Engineering and released by the Milton Bradley Company.
The Microvision did not contain an onboard processor, instead each game included it’s own processor within the removable cartridge. This meant the console consisted of the controls, LCD panel and LCD controller.
The console features a 2’’ LCD screen and 16x16 display with each pixel looking like a black block.
The Microvision had 12 games released with the 12th game, Super Blockbuster, being a Europe exclusive.

History

The Microvision had moderate success, but very few cartridges, a small screen and lack of support from established home video game companies led to it’s demise in 1981.

Hardware

  • CPU: Intel 8021/TI TMS1100 (on cartridge)
  • Screen type and resolution: 16 × 16 pixel LCD
  • Register width: 4 bit (TMS1100), 8 bit (8021)
  • Processor speed: 100 kHz
  • RAM (integrated into CPU): 64 bytes
  • ROM: 2K (TMS100), 1K (8021)
  • Cartridge ROM: 2K (TMS 1100), 1K (8021) masked (integrated into CPU; each game's CPU was different)
  • Video Display Processor: LCD Custom Driver
  • Sound: Piezo beeper
  • Input: Twelve button keypad, one paddle
  • Power requirements: One or two 9 volt batteries on earlier Microvision consoles, one 9 volt battery on later Microvision consoles
  • Power Dissipation: 110 mW (TMS 1100), 1 W (8021)
