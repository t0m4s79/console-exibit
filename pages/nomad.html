Genesis Nomad

Description

This device, also known as Sega Nomad, is a handheld game console manufactured by Sega and released in North America in 1995. It’s a portable variation of the Sega Genesis home video game console(Mega Drive outside North America). It was Sega’s second and last handheld console to be released following the Sega Game Gear.
The Nomad shares a similar concept with the semi-portable console Sega Mega Jet, a console that was also a variation of the Sega Genesis designed for use on airplane flights in Japan.
The Nomad could be used with a TV set via a video port.

History

The Nomad was designed by Sega of America and was another attempt to extend the shelf life of the already successful Sega Genesis. It was presumed to still have a place on the market due to having a 600+ game library and better hardware than the competition.
Originally was going to have a touch-screen interface but it was too expensive at the time.
It made it’s debut on some select test markets in October 1995 with an asking price of US$179. Although it received praise from critics it’s sales were low, even after a US$100 price drop. Support for Genesis and Genesis-related products was dropped since the Sega Saturn was doing better than these in Japan.
The Nomad also suffered from some issues, some which were already present in Sega’s previous handheld console, like a short battery life, higher power consumption compared to the competition, and the Nomad also didn’t support the peripherals for Genesis.

Hardware

  • Processor: Motorola 68000 16 bit processor running at 7.67 MHz
  • Co-processor (Sound Controller): Zilog Z80 8-bit at 3.58 MHz
  • Memory: 156KB total - 64 KB Main RAM, 64KB VRAM, 8KB Sound RAM. 20 Kb ROM
  • Display Palette: 512
  • Onscreen colors: 64
  • Maximum onscreen sprites: 80
  • Resolution: 320 × 224
  • Sound: Yamaha YM2612 6 channel FM, additional 4 channel PSG. Stereo sound. Also TI SN76489]] PSG (Programmable Sound Generator)
  • Display: Integrated STN LCD at 320 x 224
  • Power Rating: 9V 850mA (same as Mega Drive model 2)
  • Power requirements: One or two 9 volt batteries on earlier Microvision consoles, one 9 volt battery on later Microvision consoles
  • Power Dissipation: 110 mW (TMS 1100), 1 W (8021)
