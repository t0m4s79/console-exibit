Atari Lynx

Description

The first console with LCD screen capable of displaying color and created by one of the most famous companies in the world of gaming of the time Atari. One of the most interesting characteristic of the Lynx was her ability to be switch from right-handed to Left-handed mode by pressing two button and using the console upside down, to accomplish this feat the console has 2 A and B button for each mode, the console also in the right side there were the Option 1,Option 2 , Restart, and Flip and in the left has a D-Pad and a on and off button. This console was a worthy competitor to the Original Gameboy having more features it still didn’t win against its rival.

History

The Lynx was originally known as the Handy Game developed by Epyx but due to financial difficulties the company decided to enter a partnership with Atari where it would produce and market , and Epyx would be in charge of the software. The launch of the Atari Lynx on September 1, 1989 was a success, Atari reporting that 90% of its 50.000 units shipped in the first month were sold. In 1991 to improve sales Atari decided to redesign the console to improve the hardware, battery lifespan and give it a new sleeker look, this redesign was referred to as Lynx II.As time passed the Lynx popularity started to fall leading to the discontinuation of the console in 1995.

Hardware

  • Mikey (8-bit custom CMOS chip running at 16 MHz)
    • WDC 8-bit 65SC02 processor (based on the MOS 6502) running at up to 4 MHz (3.6 MHz average)
    • Sound engine
      • 4 channel sound
      • 8-bit DAC for each channel (4 channels × 8-bits/channel = 32 bits commonly quoted) these four sound channels can also switch in analogue sound mode to generate PSG sound . Atari reports the range is "100 Hz to above the range of human hearing"; spectrum analysis shows the range may go as low as 32 Hz
      • Stereo with panning (Lynx II PAG-0401 only, original Lynx and early Lynx II are mono)
    • Video DMA driver for liquid-crystal display
      • Custom built and designed by Jay Miner and David Morse
      • 160×102 pixels resolution
      • 4,096 color (12-bit) palette
      • 16 simultaneous colors (4 bits) from palette per scanline
      • Variable frame rate (up to 75 frames/second)
    • Eight system timers (two reserved for LCD timing, one for UART)
    • Interrupt controller
    • UART (for Comlynx) (fixed format 8E1, up to 62500 Bd)
    • 512 bytes of bootstrap and game-card loading ROM
  • Suzy (16-bit custom CMOS chip running at 16 MHz)
    • Unlimited number of blitter "sprites" with collision detection
    • Hardware sprite scaling, distortion, and tilting effects
    • Hardware decoding of compressed sprite data
    • Hardware clipping and multi-directional scrolling
    • Math engine
      • Hardware 16-bit × 16-bit → 32-bit multiply with optional accumulation; 32-bit ÷ 16-bit → 16-bit divide
      • Parallel processing of CPU
  • RAM: 64 KB 120ns DRAM
  • Cartridges of 128, 256, 512KiB and (with bank-switching) 1MiB
  • Ports:
    • Headphone port (3.5 mm stereo; wired for mono on the original Lynx)
    • ComLynx (multiple unit communications, serial)
  • LCD Screen: 3.5" diagonal
  • Battery holder (six AA) 4–5 hours (Lynx I) 5–6 hours (Lynx II)
